How did you go from Philosophy/Art History to Data Science?

I started my journey in data as an undergraduate at the University of Pennsylvania, intending to major in Economics and Statistics. Enjoyably, these subjects utilized my pattern-recognition and problem-solving skills as a means to generate tangible impact, and thus, I spent the larger part of my underclassman years gearing for a career in business intelligence and analytics.

However, around 3/5ths of the way through my undergraduate career, I discovered the exciting world of the art auction houses and the art market. Much the same with traditional data scientists, art specialists had to analytically uncover and iterate upon patterns to make impactful business decisions--only, their patterns were drawn from a more diverse pool of data (from sales histories to academic texts to visual art to even client preconceptions). Without the proper toolkit at the time to effectively find patterns in the art market, I became obsessive with connecting analytical theories to numerical and financial insights. This desire was the impetus for my sharp pivot into the areas I ultimately matriculated in: Philosophy and Art History.

Now a post-grad who has had his fill of the art market, I wish return to my original passions in data science and analysis. Hence, I immediately joined Penn's Data Science program after my Bachelors as a means to directly pursue my data science dreams.

How does a non-technical undergrad help you in a technical role within Data Science?

My "detour" into philosophy and art speciality provides me with a unique and expanded problem-solving toolkit, as I can comfortably navigate a higher range of problems and data types, adapt myself to a larger pool of problem topics, and draw upon a greater and more diverse breadth of industry experience! A humanities background also gives me a heightened story-telling capacity, as a much of the degree is about aggregating and synthesizing many disparate data sources into coherent paper conclusions.
